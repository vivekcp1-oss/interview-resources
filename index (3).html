Artificial Intelligence (AI)

The broad concept of machines executing tasks in a way we would consider "smart." This can be as simple as hard-coded `if-else` blocks.

Machine Learning (ML)

The statistical application of AI. Instead of rules, you provide historical data, and the system probabilistically determines outcomes.

Deep Learning (DL)

A sub-field of ML utilizing multi-layered neural networks, specifically excelling at handling unstructured data like images and audio.

Core Types of Machine Learning

Models are categorized by how they learn from data. Choosing the right approach depends entirely on whether your data has "labels" (an answer key) or not.

🎯

Supervised Learning

Requires labeled data (Features + Label). The model is trained on a known "answer key" to predict future outcomes.

model.fit(X_train, y_label)

Example: Predicting if a transaction is fraud.

🔍

Unsupervised Learning

Uses unlabeled data. The goal is discovery—finding hidden patterns, groupings, or structures without a specific target.

KMeans().fit(X_train)

Example: Segmenting customer behavior.

🎮

Reinforcement Learning

Learning via trial and error. An agent interacts with an environment and learns to maximize a reward signal.

agent.step(action)

Example: A bot learning to play a video game.

Comparing Model Characteristics

Different learning types prioritize different data needs and yield different levels of explanatory power. Supervised learning offers direct feedback, while Unsupervised relies entirely on algorithmic discovery.

  • ■ Supervised: High reliance on human-labeled data.
  • ■ Unsupervised: Low labeled data need, high discovery.

The Golden Rule: Data Splitting

Never evaluate your model on the same data it used for training. Doing so causes "overfitting," where the model memorizes the answers rather than learning the patterns.

Garbage In, Garbage Out (GIGO)

The quality of your predictions is strictly bound by the quality of your input data. If your historical data is biased, missing values, or heavily skewed, the world's most advanced Deep Learning model will still produce flawed results. Data cleaning is paramount.

CRITICAL MISTAKE: Data Leakage. Including test-set information in the training phase ruins the integrity of the evaluation.

Training vs. Inference Phases

Understanding the lifecycle of a model in production requires distinguishing between the heavy-lifting of training and the rapid execution of inference.

The Training Phase

Teaching the Model (Offline)

This phase uses vast amounts of historical data to adjust internal parameters. It is highly computationally expensive (requiring GPUs/TPUs) and happens offline over hours or days.

The Inference Phase

Live Predictions (Real-time)

The trained model is deployed to process new, unseen data. This requires extremely low latency to provide instant predictions to the user, using relatively lower compute resources.

Scenario: Netflix trains its recommendation engine on past viewing history globally (Training), and instantly shows you "Top Picks" the second you open the app (Inference).

Key Concepts & Interview Prep

📦

The "Black Box"

Complex models where internal logic is hidden. Inputs and outputs are visible, but the exact decision path is uninterpretable.

🎫

Feature vs Label

Features are the input variables (e.g., house size). The Label is the specific output you want to predict (e.g., price).

⚖️

Class Imbalance

Training on heavily skewed data (99% normal, 1% fraud). Models will cheat by always guessing the majority class.

📈

Scaling/Normalization

Adjusting data so large numerical values (like Salary) don't disproportionately dominate smaller values (like Age).
